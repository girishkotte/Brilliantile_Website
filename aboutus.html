Snowstorms have historically plagued many states, notoriously those located in the northeast of America. After heavy snowfall, snow needs to be removed from roofs to reduce the risk of structural damage due to weight. Shoveling entails a considerable amount of physical effort and can strain the back and heart. Each year many senior citizens and middle aged people die from heart attacks while shoveling snow. There is not an ideal automated solution that a homeowner can use to remove snow without damaging roof shingles. Therefore Brilliantile, our modular snow removal system, will be much beneficial to the community.

Manual snow removal is a very risky and labor-intensive process. More often resources are not available during severe snowstorms and emergencies due to heavy demand. Many times roof is permanently damaged in the manual removal process and replacement is a very expensive. The current technology that is used is to remove snow is heat cable. These are hot wires that run up and down the roof to melt snow and prevent ice build-up on the overhangs and valley.

Recently, another scientific technology called Pulse-Wave technology, has come up to remove snow and ice from the roof without human intervention. Vibrations are induced in the metal roofing, allowing the frozen ice or snow to slide off the roof. However, this technology is only for metal roofs and is not much in the market. Leaving the option of manually plowing snow from the roof top, it is clearly evident that automated technological improvement is needed in this area of snow removal, and it is quite surprising that there has been no advancement until now in the area that affects billions of people every year.

Presently, there is no other automated solution competing with Brilliantile. IoT and solar panels are very recent and both of these are now picking up in the market. We started our research by looking into FEMA snow load guide, snow load calculator, and diving deep into green roof design. For our prototype, we used the load sensor from a weighing scale, and then combined few of these sensors and converted them into Wheatstone configuration by using a combinator board.

During the design process, the coil was pasted on multiple surfaces to measure the heating and cooling performance and readings were taken. The coil was first pasted on the bottom surface of the glass, where we were able to attain the desired temperature, but as the coil was exposed to ambient, it cooled down faster. Then, the coil was pasted on the top of the metal, where we were able to achieve the desired temperature exceedingly fast, but metal, being a good conductor of electricity, cooled down as equally fast. We also tested the coil sandwiched between the glass and Silicon rubber. We observed the best performance with this setup.
