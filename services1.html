Bio-Medical Engineering

We assist in building cognitive data models for bio medical research institutes. Recently, we built data models for brain waves data. The cognitive descriptive, diagnostic and predictive data models help predict the teenager behavior.

Rather than depending on predefined rules and structured queries to uncover answers, cognitive analytics relies on systems that draw from a wide variety of potentially relevant information and connections to generate hypotheses. It expresses possible answers as recommendations, and includes the system’s own ranking of how confident it is in their accuracy. This process differs markedly from traditional analysis in that as more data is fed into a machine learning system, the system learns, which results in higher-quality insights and more accurate hypotheses.

The real value of cognitive analytics lies in its ability to process and understand exploding volumes of data in real time—including data that may contain wide variations of format, structure, and quality. It forms context-based hypotheses by exploring massive numbers of permutations of potential relationships, of influence, and causality, which generates conclusions unconstrained by organizational biases.

In academia, these same techniques have been applied in studies of reading, learning, and language development, such as the Never-Ending Language Learning¹ projects. In the consumer world, pieces of cognitive analytics form the core of artificial personal assistant technologies that are frequently found in mobile phones and other consumer devices.

Health care industry is deploying cognitive analytics capabilities to improve the quality of patient outcomes. By coupling a wide range of structured inputs—such as claims records, patient files, and disease outbreak statistics—with unstructured inputs like medical journals and textbooks, clinician notes, and social media feeds, doctors are able to create more detailed, accurate diagnoses that draw upon the latest medical research, statistics, and individual patient histories.

The financial services sector is using cognitive analytics to advise clients, execute trades, and detect fraud, among other activities. Retailers are beginning to use cognitive systems as virtual customer service agents and digital store clerks that can provide answers to customers’ questions about products, trends, recommendations, and support. More broadly, global organizations are deploying cognitive analytics to fine-tune complex supply chains and cloud networks.

Healthcare

Healthcare providers seek to significantly improve the standard of care they deliver. They aim to consistently incentivize best practices across all networks of care, identify and implement evidence-based medicine, reduce readmission rates, and deliver real-time reporting of infectious diseases.Providers are also investing in the promise of personalized medicine by updating their technology with unified data integration of genomic and clinical data, entity extraction, and natural language processing of clinical notes.

Significant obstacles block providers from achieving their vision. The scale of the data, variable formats, and disconnected locations of critical healthcare information make unified analysis a significant challenge. Insufficient and inflexible data integration prevents the resolution and fusion of patient identification and records. Device, clinical, pharmaceutical, claims, accounting, and scheduling data all need to be integrated as well. On the regulatory side, providers lack the tools to provide meaningful use compliance, reporting, and health information exchanges.
